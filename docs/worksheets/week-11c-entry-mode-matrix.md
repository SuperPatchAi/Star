| Mode | Definition | Investment | Risk | Control | Speed |

## Part 2: Detailed Mode Analysis

### Mode 1: Export

**How It Works:** You manufacture/provide service from home and ship/deliver to the target market.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Lowest investment | Higher shipping costs |
| Quick to start | Limited local presence |
| Easy to exit | Import duties/tariffs |
| Maintain control | Slower delivery times |

**Best For:**
- Testing market demand
- Low-volume, high-margin products
- Digital products/services
- Markets with easy import regulations

### Mode 2: Licensing/Franchising

**How It Works:** A local partner operates under your brand, paying you royalties.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Low capital required | Less quality control |
| Leverage local expertise | Potential brand risk |
| Fast market entry | Share of profits with partner |
| Partner bears operational risk | Finding right partner is hard |

**Best For:**
- Proven, replicable business model
- Strong brand value
- Markets where local knowledge is critical
- When you lack capital for direct investment

### Mode 3: Joint Venture

**How It Works:** You and a local partner create a new shared entity.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Share investment and risk | Shared decision-making |
| Local partner knowledge | Potential conflicts |
| May be required by regulations | Complex legal structure |
| Faster than subsidiary | Exit can be complicated |

**Best For:**
- Markets requiring local ownership
- When local partner brings critical assets
- Complex regulatory environments
- Large capital requirements

### Mode 4: Wholly-Owned Subsidiary

**How It Works:** You establish and fully own a local company.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Full control | Highest investment |
| All profits kept | Highest risk |
| Build local presence | Slowest to establish |
| Long-term commitment | Complex to exit |

**Best For:**
- Strategic priority markets
- When full control is essential
